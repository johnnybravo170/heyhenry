# Customer Archetypes — Small Canadian General Contractor / Renovation Business Owners

**Market:** Canadian small general-contractor / renovation businesses, roughly 1–15 people, owner-operators and small teams who could buy field-management software.  
**Date:** 2026-05-22  
**Use case:** HeyHenry positioning, Voice-of-Customer, segmentation, sales-message testing.

## Evidence notes and limits

This segmentation blends **observed archetypes** from public software reviews, contractor forums, Reddit threads, vendor positioning, and Canadian construction/SME statistics with **inferred archetypes** that are likely underrepresented online.

Important grounding signals:

- Canadian construction is structurally small-business-heavy. ISED reports that in 2025, **61.9% of Canadian construction employer establishments had 1–4 employees** and **37.0% had 5–99 employees**. Source: ISED Canadian Industry Statistics, Construction, 2025.
- Statistics Canada reports that residential construction firms with **fewer than 20 workers accounted for 66.1% of total employment in residential construction in 2023**, and that productivity declined sharply in the sector from 2001–2023. Source: Statistics Canada, “Firm size and labour productivity growth in Canadian residential construction,” 2026.
- Canadian small businesses with 1–19 employees are under pressure from inflation, insurance, input costs, recruiting, interest rates, and demand fluctuation. Source: Statistics Canada, “Analysis on small businesses in Canada, fourth quarter of 2025.”
- Digital adoption is rising, but uneven. CFIB’s 2025 SME digital transformation report found Canadian SMEs report productivity gains from digital tools, but construction remains uneven and low-maturity relative to more digital sectors. Sources: CFIB 2025 digital transformation report; ISED SME AI Adoption Blueprint.
- Online data overrepresents contractors already considering software. Reddit and Capterra data are biased toward people with enough digital fluency to search, post, review, or compare tools.
- “Spouse/back-office operator” is not a stereotype invented from thin air: public contractor/homeowner discussions include direct references to wives/bookkeepers running the back end and finances. This is a meaningful buying-center pattern, but the prevalence is inferred.

## Archetype 1 — The Growing Systems Seeker
**Tagline:** “Excel and QuickBooks got me here. They won’t get me to the next level.”

**evidence_basis:** observed  
**confidence:** high

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **12–18%** of targetable 1–15 person GC/renovation shops. Confidence: **medium**. They are highly visible online because they actively ask for software recommendations, compare platforms, and post when their current setup breaks.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
Usually 3–12 people, often one owner plus a lead carpenter/site lead, subs, and part-time admin/bookkeeping. Revenue often around **$500K–$3M**, sometimes higher if project-based remodel/custom work is healthy. Trade mix: remodels, additions, basements, decks, kitchens, small custom builds, design-build. Common across BC, Ontario, Alberta, and growing suburban markets where renovation demand is strong.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They want to move from “I’m personally holding the business together” to a repeatable operating system: estimating, change orders, client communication, job costing, scheduling, document storage, and QuickBooks flow.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
They feel it sharply. Jobs are getting bigger, customers expect faster communication, and the owner is losing evenings to admin. The specific wound is **growth without control**: more work, more revenue, but less confidence that the numbers, subs, and commitments are under control.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
A stable, professional renovation company that can run multiple jobs without the owner being the bottleneck. They want margin visibility, fewer surprises, better client experience, and eventually a business that is not just “the owner with a tool belt and a phone.”

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They believe software is necessary, but only if it matches construction reality. They are open to systems and best practices, but they hate generic SaaS that feels designed by someone who has never had a homeowner change tile mid-project.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
Too expensive; too much setup; too complex for a small crew; subs will not use it; field guys will ignore it; QuickBooks sync will become a mess; the demo looks good but daily use will not; they will be trapped in a contract; they will spend months building templates and never finish.

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
A live workflow from lead → estimate → signed proposal → job → change order → invoice → QuickBooks. Canadian tax/holdback support. Real renovation examples, not HVAC/service-call demos. Proof that a 5-person team can adopt it without a full-time operations manager. Migration help. A trial or pilot on one live job.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
They win a larger project than usual; hire their first admin; miss a change order; lose track of job costs; get burned by a vague paper trail; become frustrated with Excel/QuickBooks; spouse/bookkeeper says the current system is not sustainable.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner leads, but spouse/bookkeeper/office manager heavily influences. Decision criteria: job costing, estimating templates, change orders, client communication, QuickBooks integration, mobile usability, implementation support, monthly cost, and how easily crew/subs can participate.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Reddit construction/software threads, Facebook contractor groups, YouTube comparisons, Capterra/G2 reviews, bookkeeper/CPA recommendations, other local contractors, CHBA/HBA peers, trade association contacts.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“Getting away from Excel and QuickBooks.” “I need invoicing, payments, estimating, client info, sub insurance.” “Buildertrend would fit the bill but I’m not trying to spend $800 a month.” “I need something that does not turn into a full-time job.” “One place for everything.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
“AI-powered transformation” without showing the actual workflow. Enterprise sales pressure. Hiding pricing. Demoing features for HVAC/service dispatch instead of renovation projects. Acting like every crew member will happily log into a portal ten times a day.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
They will pay if the ROI is clear, but **$500–$1,000/month feels like a serious management decision**, not a casual SaaS subscription. They think in terms of “one saved change order,” “one avoided admin hire,” or “one fewer Saturday doing paperwork.”

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“We’re at that awkward size where QuickBooks and spreadsheets technically still work, but I’m the only reason they work. I don’t need Procore. I need something that helps me quote faster, track changes, keep subs and customers straight, and not spend my nights fixing admin. If it takes six months to set up or my guys won’t touch it, I’m out.”

## Archetype 2 — The Burned-by-Software Skeptic
**Tagline:** “I already tried the ‘all-in-one’ thing. It became one more problem.”

**evidence_basis:** observed  
**confidence:** high

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **8–14%** of the market. Confidence: **medium**. Strongly represented in reviews and forums because dissatisfied software buyers post warnings and comparison notes.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
Usually 4–15 people, established enough to have tried Jobber, Buildertrend, CoConstruct, Housecall Pro, ServiceTitan, Buildxact, Contractor Foreman, or similar. Revenue roughly **$750K–$5M**. Mix of remodeling, design-build, specialty exterior/interior work, and sometimes service-adjacent trade work.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They want to regain operational control without repeating a bad software purchase. Their real job-to-be-done is **de-risking change**.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
They feel pain, but they also feel scar tissue. They may be stuck in a system that is clunky, expensive, missing renovation-specific flexibility, or no longer improving. The pain is not only admin; it is **loss of trust**.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
They want a simpler, more reliable operating backbone. They want their team to stop complaining about the system. They want fewer workarounds, fewer duplicate entries, and fewer “support ticket black holes.”

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They believe software companies overpromise. They believe demos are choreographed. They believe pricing gets worse once you are locked in. They believe “new features” often means “they moved the button again.”

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
“We’ve heard this before.” “Support disappears after the sale.” “The mobile app will be worse than desktop.” “The accounting sync will break.” “They’ll keep adding features instead of fixing basics.” “My data will be trapped.” “We’ll spend money and still use texts and spreadsheets.”

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Blunt comparison against their current tool. Honest acknowledgement of limitations. Month-to-month option or clean cancellation. Migration plan. References from contractors who switched from the same platform. Transparent roadmap. Support access before purchase. A rep who says “no, we don’t do that yet” instead of hand-waving.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
Price increase, failed support incident, software change that breaks workflow, accounting sync issue, bad customer experience, owner realizes the team has abandoned the system and gone back to texting.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner plus the person who suffered most under the last system: office manager, spouse/bookkeeper, estimator, or project manager. Decision criteria: support quality, ease of migration, fewer clicks, reliability, pricing transparency, and ability to run a real-world pilot.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Reddit threads, Capterra negative reviews, Facebook groups, peer-to-peer calls, YouTube “why I left X” videos, bookkeeper networks.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“Overkill for me.” “It adds up quick.” “Glorified spreadsheet with a nice UI.” “They keep implementing new features instead of fixing flaws.” “Not built for project-based construction.” “I would only recommend this to my competition.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
Aggressive SaaS sales. “All-in-one” claims. AI buzzwords. Annual contracts. Setup fees. Hidden pricing. Feature dumps. Claiming migration will be “easy” without specifics.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
Moderate to high. They may pay for the right system, but only after trust is rebuilt. They compare total cost: subscription + setup + training + lost time + risk of switching again.

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“We already bought the shiny system once. The demo looked great, then six months later we had three workarounds, the guys hated it, and support kept telling us it was on the roadmap. I’m not against software. I’m against paying someone to create a second job for me.”

## Archetype 3 — The Paper-and-Phone Holdout
**Tagline:** “My notebook doesn’t crash, and my phone still rings.”

**evidence_basis:** inferred  
**confidence:** medium-high

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **15–25%** of the total market, but much less reachable through digital ads and forums. Confidence: **medium**. This is likely one of the largest under-observed groups because they are not posting in software threads.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
Often 1–5 people. Revenue roughly **$150K–$1M**, but some high-reputation small crews can exceed that. Years in business often 10–30+. Trade mix: small renovations, repairs, decks, bathrooms, basements, carpentry-heavy work, rural and suburban projects. Strong in smaller towns, rural areas, older contractor networks, and word-of-mouth-heavy local markets.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They are not consciously hiring software. They are hiring **relief from lost notes, forgotten follow-ups, and late-night paperwork**, but they may not frame it that way.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
Often the pain is normalized. They forget to invoice, rely on memory, lose paper measurements, miss callbacks, or delay quotes, but see it as “part of the job.” They feel acute pain only when it costs money or embarrasses them.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
Keep steady work, stay respected locally, avoid headaches, get paid, avoid hiring office staff, and maybe slow down without chaos. They may not want to “scale.”

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They believe good work and reputation matter more than software. They see SaaS as something for bigger companies, younger guys, or “office people.” They value practical tools, not platforms.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
“I don’t have time to learn that.” “My guys won’t use it.” “I don’t want another monthly bill.” “I’m not typing into an app all day.” “I don’t need a CRM.” “I’ve been doing this 25 years.” “What happens when I have no signal?” “I don’t want my customers in some portal.”

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Voice-first input. No jargon. Very low setup burden. Local contractor proof. A “use it like leaving yourself a voicemail” workflow. A spouse/bookkeeper endorsement. Simple before/after: fewer forgotten invoices, faster quotes, fewer callbacks lost.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
A missed invoice; a customer complaint about communication; spouse/bookkeeper frustration; tax-season chaos; losing a job because they quoted too slowly; owner health/age forcing less mental load; hiring first helper.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner may resist; spouse/bookkeeper often pushes. Decision criteria: simplicity, voice input, no setup nightmare, phone-based workflow, no mandatory desktop work, low monthly cost, no contract.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Other local contractors, suppliers, lumberyards, accountants/bookkeepers, family members, Facebook community groups, trade associations, phone calls, in-person referrals. They may not read reviews unless someone sends them a link.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“I’ve got it in my book.” “Text me the address.” “I’ll send the quote when I get home.” “I forgot to bill that one.” “My wife handles the paperwork.” “I’m not a computer guy.” “I just need to remember who I promised what to.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
CRM, workflow automation, AI-native OS, dashboards, implementation, “digital transformation,” enterprise language, long onboarding, anything that implies they are behind or dumb.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
High. They think in cash-flow terms: “Is this worth another phone bill?” A $50–$150/month tool can be acceptable if tied to getting paid faster or avoiding one missed invoice. Anything that feels like a “platform” sounds expensive.

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“I’m not sitting at a laptop after ten hours on site just to feed some program. I’ve got my notebook, my phone, and people know how to reach me. Now, if I could talk into my phone and it remembered what I said for later, maybe that’s different. But I’m not signing up for some big software project.”

**What would have to be true**  
They have enough admin leakage to justify software, but not enough digital confidence to shop proactively. A voice-first, low-friction tool would feel different from “software.”

**How to validate it**  
Sales-call question: “Where do job notes, customer promises, and change requests live today?”  
Survey item: “How often do you delay invoicing or quoting because the details are in texts, notebooks, or memory?”

## Archetype 4 — The Spouse-Run Back Office
**Tagline:** “He builds it. I keep the business from catching fire.”

**evidence_basis:** inferred / partially observed  
**confidence:** high for existence, medium for prevalence

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **10–18%** of the market. Confidence: **medium**. Direct online references show wives/spouses/bookkeepers running finances/back-end functions, but exact prevalence is not publicly measured.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
Usually 2–10 people. Owner is field-heavy; spouse, partner, or family member handles bookkeeping, invoicing, payroll, scheduling, customer emails, receipts, and taxes. Revenue often **$300K–$2M**. Common in family-run renovation, carpentry, deck, basement, and small design-build businesses across Canada.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
The buyer wants to **extract business information from the owner’s head and phone** so the back office can invoice, schedule, follow up, and reconcile without chasing him all night.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
The back-office person feels it intensely. The owner may not. Pain includes incomplete job notes, missing receipts, late invoices, unclear change orders, customer messages scattered across texts/calls, and bookkeeping cleanup.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
Less stress at home, cleaner books, faster billing, fewer “did you remember?” conversations, and a business that does not consume evenings and weekends. Some want growth; many just want sanity.

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They believe systems matter because they live with the consequences of no system. They are pragmatic: software must reduce chasing, not create new admin. They may be more digitally comfortable than the owner.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
“He won’t use it.” “The crew won’t enter things.” “I’ll end up managing the software too.” “QuickBooks integration better not make my books worse.” “We don’t have time to set it up.” “The app needs to work from the truck.”

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Voice-to-admin workflows, receipt capture, job notes converted into tasks/invoices, clean QuickBooks sync, spouse/bookkeeper-facing demos, proof that the field owner only has to talk or tap, not manage screens.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
Tax season mess; missed invoice; cash-flow crunch; spouse burnout; owner forgets a customer commitment; hiring employees; bookkeeper threatens to raise rates or quits cleaning up chaos.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
The spouse/back-office person may be the real champion or blocker. Decision criteria: QuickBooks fit, invoice/change-order workflow, receipt capture, customer communication history, simple field adoption, and support quality.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Bookkeeping groups, QuickBooks communities, Facebook contractor-spouse groups, local accountants, other contractor spouses, Capterra reviews, YouTube tutorials, vendor help docs.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“I do the books.” “I’m always chasing him for receipts.” “He forgot to invoice.” “Everything is in his texts.” “I need to know what changed on the job.” “I’m the one cleaning it up.” “I just need him to put it somewhere I can find it.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
Field-only messaging that ignores admin reality. “Your crew will fill everything out perfectly.” Weak accounting answers. Any implication that the owner alone is the buyer.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
Moderate. They compare cost to bookkeeping hours, late invoices, cash-flow delays, and marital/home stress. They may justify software faster than the owner if it clearly reduces chasing.

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“He’ll remember every cut he made on a job, but somehow not where the receipt went. I’m not looking for fancy. I need notes, receipts, customer changes, and invoices in one place so I’m not digging through texts at 10 p.m. If he can talk into his phone and I can turn that into admin, I’ll listen.”

**What would have to be true**  
In many small contractor businesses, the software buyer is not always the field owner; the operational pain is carried by a spouse, bookkeeper, or office admin.

**How to validate it**  
Sales-call question: “Who currently handles invoices, receipts, bookkeeping, and customer follow-up?”  
Survey item: “Who feels the most pain when job notes or receipts are missing: owner, spouse/bookkeeper, office manager, or customer?”

## Archetype 5 — The Too-Busy-to-Post Silent Majority
**Tagline:** “I know the system is a mess. I just don’t have a spare week to fix it.”

**evidence_basis:** inferred  
**confidence:** medium

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **15–22%**. Confidence: **medium-low** because they are defined partly by absence from public discussion. Indirect signals include high workload, small-firm dominance, labour/input pressure, and the common contractor pattern of delayed quotes/invoices/admin.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
3–10 people, project backlog, owner still active in field or daily supervision. Revenue roughly **$500K–$2.5M**. Mix of renovations, additions, decks, kitchens, bathrooms, exterior improvements. Often in high-demand metro/suburban areas.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They need to **reduce admin load without stopping production**. They are not shopping for software; they need a system that can be adopted in motion.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
They feel overwhelmed, but software research falls behind today’s fire. Pain shows up as slow quotes, late callbacks, under-documented changes, missed follow-ups, cash-flow lag, and constant mental load.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
Finish current jobs without chaos, keep customers happy, protect reputation, maybe hire one admin or project lead later. They want breathing room more than “growth hacking.”

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They are not anti-tech. They are time-starved. They believe a system could help, but they assume evaluating and implementing it will be painful.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
“No time.” “Call me in winter.” “We’re buried right now.” “I can’t switch systems mid-job.” “I don’t have my pricing/templates organized.” “I need to get through this project first.” “I don’t want to train the crew.”

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Done-with-you onboarding. “Start with one job.” Voice capture. No migration requirement on day one. Fast time-to-value. Mobile-first. Clear promise: get your current chaos into one place this week, not rebuild the company.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
Seasonal slowdown, winter admin catch-up, losing a lead due to slow response, customer frustration, hiring a helper/admin, bookkeeping deadline, project dispute over undocumented scope.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner decides, but spouse/bookkeeper may push. They require low adoption friction, immediate benefit, and no interruption to current jobs.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Peers, suppliers, bookkeepers, short YouTube demos, Facebook groups, direct referrals. They may not browse long review threads.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“I’m buried.” “I’ll look at it when things slow down.” “Everything is in my head right now.” “I just need to get through this job.” “I’m behind on quotes.” “I haven’t billed that yet.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
Long demos, “implementation journey,” full migration plans, enterprise dashboards, requiring perfect data upfront, sales follow-up that creates more work.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
Moderate. They will pay for relief if it is immediate. Their bigger cost is attention. A free trial is less persuasive than “we’ll set up your first job with you.”

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“I’m not against getting organized. I’m just running three jobs, pricing two more, and answering customers between site visits. If your setup needs me to sit at a computer for a week, it’s not happening. Show me how it helps today without me stopping the train.”

**What would have to be true**  
The primary barrier is not skepticism or price, but implementation bandwidth.

**How to validate it**  
Sales-call question: “What is the main reason you have not changed your admin system yet: cost, trust, time, crew adoption, or not feeling the pain?”  
Survey item: “Would you be more likely to adopt software if the first live job was set up for you?”

## Archetype 6 — The Craft-First Reputation Builder
**Tagline:** “My business is my name. Don’t make me look like a franchise.”

**evidence_basis:** inferred / partially observed  
**confidence:** medium

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **8–13%**. Confidence: **medium**. This archetype is visible indirectly through premium renovation positioning, homeowner review language, and contractor emphasis on trust, workmanship, and communication.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
1–8 people, often owner-led, high-referral, project quality over volume. Revenue **$250K–$2M**, sometimes higher with premium renovation/design-build. Region: affluent suburbs and urban renovation markets such as Vancouver, Victoria, Calgary, GTA, Ottawa, Kelowna, Montreal suburbs.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They want to look professional without becoming impersonal. The tool’s job is to protect reputation: timely communication, clean proposals, documented changes, polished client experience.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
They may not feel operational pain daily if they intentionally stay small. But they feel pain when client communication slips, when scope changes are undocumented, or when premium clients expect a more polished process.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
Premium referrals, better clients, less price shopping, selective projects, controlled workload, and a reputation for trust and craftsmanship.

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They believe trust is earned personally. Software should support relationship, not replace it. They dislike anything that makes them look automated, cheap, spammy, or overly corporate.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
“My clients hire me, not a portal.” “I don’t want automated messages sounding robotic.” “This looks like service-company software.” “I don’t need to scale volume.” “My process is personal.” “Will this make my communication feel canned?”

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Beautiful client-facing proposals, tasteful communication, configurable language, photo/document sharing, professional change orders, client timeline, and examples from premium remodelers. The tone must feel human.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
Higher-end project; client asks for better visibility; dispute over change order; desire to raise prices; moving from handyman/small jobs into larger renovations; hiring first office admin.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner decides, sometimes with designer, spouse, or office admin. Criteria: client-facing polish, customization, minimal friction, photo/document handling, communication log, proposal quality.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Peer referrals, Instagram/portfolio-driven contractors, local builder associations, supplier relationships, designers/architects, homeowner review platforms, YouTube when researching specific tools.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“I don’t want to look like a truck-and-ladder franchise.” “Clients expect updates.” “I need everything documented.” “I want the proposal to look professional.” “My reputation is everything.” “I don’t chase volume.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
Aggressive growth language, “book more jobs,” spammy automation, contractor-bro sales tone, ugly templates, generic AI copy, low-end service-business examples.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
Moderate-low if it supports premium positioning. They compare the cost to one avoided dispute, one better client experience, or one higher-ticket job.

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“I’m not trying to become the biggest renovation company in town. I want the right clients, clean communication, and no awkward conversations about what was included. If software helps me look organized and protects the relationship, great. If it makes me sound like a call centre, no thanks.”

**What would have to be true**  
Premium small contractors buy software less for efficiency language and more for reputation protection and client experience.

**How to validate it**  
Sales-call question: “Which matters more to you: getting more leads, reducing admin time, protecting margin, or creating a more professional client experience?”  
Survey item: “Would you pay for software primarily because it improves how clients experience your company?”

## Archetype 7 — The Price-Squeezed Survivalist
**Tagline:** “Margins are thin, customers are jumpy, and every monthly bill has to justify itself.”

**evidence_basis:** observed / inferred  
**confidence:** medium-high

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **12–20%**. Confidence: **medium**. Supported by small-business pressure signals around inflation, insurance, input costs, interest rates, and software-cost complaints in forums.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
1–8 people, revenue **$150K–$1.5M**, often in competitive renovation/repair markets. Trade mix includes general renovation, handyman-to-GC transition, decks, fences, basements, bathrooms, small commercial maintenance. More common where customers are price-sensitive or lead flow is uneven.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They want to stop leaks: faster invoicing, fewer missed payments, better estimates, less underpricing, clearer change orders. They are not buying “management software”; they are buying **margin protection**.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
They feel cash pressure. Pain includes underquoting, material price changes, slow-paying customers, unpaid change work, insurance/tax/admin costs, and quote-shopping customers.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
Stay profitable, avoid debt, get paid faster, stop eating costs, become selective enough to say no to bad jobs.

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They are wary of recurring costs. They believe many tools are priced for bigger businesses. They judge everything by immediate utility.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
“I don’t make enough for that.” “Another monthly bill.” “I can do that in a spreadsheet.” “I’m not paying per user.” “The setup fee makes no sense.” “Will this actually get me paid faster?” “Will I have to increase prices just to cover the software?”

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Whole-dollar pricing, no per-seat trap, clear ROI examples, month-to-month plan, faster-payment proof, change-order recovery examples, simple pricing calculator: “recover one missed change order and it pays for X months.”

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
Cash-flow crunch, unpaid invoice, cost overrun, customer dispute, accountant/bookkeeper warning, insurance/tax bill, material price swing, growth beyond what spreadsheets can manage.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner plus spouse/bookkeeper. Criteria: price, no hidden fees, no per-seat penalty, fast billing, change-order capture, payment collection, and integration with existing bookkeeping.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Reddit pricing comparisons, Capterra, peers, accountants/bookkeepers, YouTube, local Facebook groups, supplier counter conversations.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“I don’t make enough for that.” “It adds up quick.” “Overkill for me.” “I need to get paid.” “I can’t eat that cost.” “Customers think everything is included.” “I need to know if I’m making money on the job.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
Hidden pricing, per-seat pricing, setup fees, enterprise comparisons, “invest in transformation,” annual-only contracts, upsell-heavy demos.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
High. They think in terms of cash leaving the account every month. The winning frame is not “software cost,” but “admin leakage / margin leakage / missed-billing insurance.”

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“I’m not paying hundreds a month just so a salesperson can say I’m more organized. Show me where it saves money or helps me collect money. If it stops one change order from disappearing, fine, now we’re talking. But I’m not adding another bill because it has a nice dashboard.”

## Archetype 8 — The Crew-Control Foreman-Owner
**Tagline:** “The office is not the problem. Getting the field to actually follow the plan is the problem.”

**evidence_basis:** observed / inferred  
**confidence:** medium

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **7–12%**. Confidence: **medium**. Observed in discussions focused on scheduling, crew coordination, daily logs, photos, time tracking, RFIs/change orders, and field adoption.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
5–15 people, owner may no longer be on tools full-time but is still deeply involved in site coordination. Revenue **$1M–$4M**. Trade mix: renovations, additions, exteriors, multi-week/multi-month projects, specialty construction, small commercial fit-outs.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They want field execution visibility: who is where, what got done, what changed, what materials are missing, which photos prove progress, and what needs action tomorrow.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
They feel pain when jobs stall because information lives in conversations. Crew forgets photos, subs miss schedule changes, materials are not ready, and the owner has to babysit every site.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
Run multiple jobs with less owner supervision, build reliable crews/leads, reduce rework, know daily progress without driving everywhere, and professionalize field communication.

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They believe systems are useless unless the field uses them. They are skeptical of office-heavy tools. They want practical mobile workflows that survive dirty hands, bad weather, spotty signal, and impatient trades.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
“My guys won’t use it.” “Subs won’t log in.” “Too many clicks.” “The app better be fast.” “No one is filling out daily logs.” “We already text.” “The field won’t care about dashboards.”

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Push-to-talk notes, photo capture, simple daily log, offline/low-friction mobile, role-based access, no-login subcontractor/client sharing, SMS-based updates, proof from small crews.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
Multiple simultaneous jobs, missed site instruction, rework due to bad communication, crew growth, new lead hand, customer asks for progress updates, owner tired of driving site-to-site.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner, lead hand/foreman, sometimes office admin. Criteria: mobile speed, crew adoption, photo/log workflow, schedule visibility, task clarity, text/SMS integration, no complicated permissions for every sub.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Crew peers, contractor forums, YouTube demos, Facebook groups, tool comparisons, field-service software discussions, local builders.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“My guys won’t use it.” “Everything turns into a text thread.” “I need photos from the site.” “Who’s actually there today?” “No more chasing updates.” “I need to know what got done.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
Desktop-first software, admin-heavy onboarding, dashboards with no field workflow, portals that require subs to create accounts, “just have your crew enter it” assumptions.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
Moderate. They will pay if field adoption reduces rework, travel, and project delays. They resist paying for office modules they will not use.

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“The problem isn’t making a schedule in the office. The problem is getting the change from Tuesday morning to actually reach the guy on site before he does it wrong. If the crew has to click through five screens, forget it. Give me photos, notes, and updates from the field without turning my guys into data-entry clerks.”

## Archetype 9 — The Software-Forward Optimizer
**Tagline:** “If the workflow is clean, I’ll adopt it before my competitors do.”

**evidence_basis:** observed  
**confidence:** high

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **5–9%**. Confidence: **medium**. They are overrepresented online because they compare tools, post detailed reviews, discuss integrations, and adopt new platforms early.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
3–15 people, often ambitious and more process-oriented. Revenue **$750K–$5M+**. Trade mix: renovation, design-build, custom work, specialty contracting, sometimes builder/remodeler hybrid. More visible in urban/suburban growth markets.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They want operational leverage: templates, automation, clean client portal, QBO sync, job costing, reporting, AI-assisted estimating/admin, and scalable process.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
They feel pain proactively. They may not be on fire, but they see inefficiency as lost margin and growth ceiling. They want to fix process before chaos forces them.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
Best-run small contractor in their market. More professional than competitors. Better margins, better client experience, faster quoting, less manual admin, data-driven decisions.

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They believe adoption creates advantage. They like tools if the design is strong and the company is responsive. They may enjoy customization and continuous improvement.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
Not customizable enough; lacks API/integrations; weak mobile; slow feature development; poor support; data lock-in; not robust enough for estimating/job costing; AI is superficial; Canadian accounting/tax gaps.

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Fast roadmap, transparent changelog, strong support community, API/integrations, QuickBooks Online sync, polished UX, customizable workflows, AI that performs real admin tasks, not just chatbot fluff.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
New business planning cycle, hiring admin/PM, switching accounting workflows, frustration with current tool limitations, desire to standardize before scaling, seeing a peer outperform.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner is usually primary, sometimes with estimator, PM, or bookkeeper. Criteria: workflow depth, customization, integrations, reporting, UX quality, support responsiveness, future product direction.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Reddit, YouTube software comparisons, vendor communities, Facebook power-user groups, Capterra/G2, peer operators, podcasts, newsletters, AI/productivity communities.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“Customizable.” “QBO integration.” “Client portal.” “Templates.” “Workflow.” “Automations.” “Data in one place.” “Still learning more every day.” “Nimble.” “Polished UI.”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
Shallow AI, weak integrations, no roadmap, closed systems, slow support, ugly UX, no export options, generic “for contractors” claims without showing depth.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
Moderate-low if product quality is high. They view software as leverage, but they benchmark aggressively and dislike pricing that punishes team growth.

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“I’m fine paying for software if it actually becomes the operating layer for the business. I want clean estimating, QBO sync, templates, client comms, and data I can trust. But don’t show me a chatbot and call it AI. Show me the workflow it eliminates.”

## Archetype 10 — The Service-Work Hybrid GC
**Tagline:** “Some days we’re a renovation company. Some days we’re a service company.”

**evidence_basis:** observed / inferred  
**confidence:** medium

**Prevalence — rough share of the market + how confident you are**  
Estimated **6–11%**. Confidence: **medium**. Supported by overlap in software categories where Jobber/Housecall Pro serve home service trades, while GCs/remodelers compare whether those tools fit project-based work.

**Firmographics — crew size, revenue band, trade mix, years in business, region**  
2–12 people. Revenue **$300K–$2M**. Mix includes handyman-to-GC, repair/maintenance plus small renovations, exterior work, decks/fences, rental/property-manager jobs, punch-list work, small remodels. More common in local service markets and among companies transitioning upward.

**Job-to-be-done — what they're really hiring a tool to accomplish**  
They need one system that can handle both quick jobs and longer projects: scheduling, estimates, dispatch-like tasks, invoices, repeat customers, photos, and simple project tracking.

**Bleeding-neck pain — the urgent, painful problem (and whether they even feel it)**  
They feel pain from workflow mismatch. Pure construction tools feel too heavy; service tools feel too dispatch-oriented and weak for multi-week project costing/change orders.

**Goals & aspirations — where they want their business to be**  
Either grow into larger renovation work, or build a reliable mixed business with repeat maintenance/service revenue plus higher-ticket projects.

**Worldview & beliefs — how they see software, salespeople, "the way things should work"**  
They are practical and comparison-driven. They may like Jobber/HCP simplicity but worry those tools are not built for project-based construction.

**Objections & skepticisms — every reason they'd say no**  
“Is this for service calls or projects?” “I don’t need enterprise construction software.” “I do not want a system that only works for HVAC-style dispatch.” “Can it handle change orders?” “Can it handle small jobs without overkill?”

**Trust signals — what actually earns their belief**  
Flexible job types, quick estimate/invoice flow, simple scheduling, project folders, change orders, recurring customers, no-login customer communication, and examples for handyman → remodeler growth path.

**Buying triggers — the event that makes them finally act**  
Volume increases; repeat customers/property managers grow; service jobs interrupt project work; missed follow-ups; owner wants to move upmarket; cash-flow issues from delayed invoicing.

**Decision criteria & who else decides (spouse, office manager, etc.)**  
Owner plus office admin/spouse. Criteria: simplicity, mixed workflow fit, scheduling, mobile estimates/invoices, customer records, change orders, payments, and price.

**Information sources — where they hang out and who they trust**  
Jobber/HCP reviews, handyman/contractor forums, Reddit, Facebook groups, YouTube reviews, peers in adjacent trades, local property managers.

**Vocabulary — actual words/phrases they use for their problems (verbatim style)**  
“Jobber seems more for service companies.” “Housecall Pro is for HVAC/plumbing.” “I need something simple.” “We do small jobs and bigger renos.” “I don’t want overkill.” “Can it handle both?”

**What makes them recoil — hype, jargon, tactics that instantly lose them**  
Too much construction-enterprise complexity, too much service-dispatch language, rigid workflows, pricing that assumes a big office, or anything that forces them to choose one business model.

**Price sensitivity & mental model of cost**  
Moderate-high. They like accessible monthly pricing, trials, and fast setup. They will pay more if it helps them climb into larger projects without losing small-job efficiency.

**Voice sample — 3-4 sentences written as that person would actually talk**  
“We’re not exactly HVAC dispatch, but we’re not building custom homes either. We’ve got small jobs, repeat customers, and then a bathroom or basement that turns into a real project. I need something simple enough for quick work but not useless when a job runs three weeks and changes twice.”

## Strategic takeaway

For HeyHenry, the strongest beachhead is probably not “all small contractors.” It is:

1. **Growing Systems Seekers** who know they need a system but reject enterprise pricing/complexity.
2. **Spouse-Run Back Offices** where the admin pain is acute and voice-first field capture solves a real household/business problem.
3. **Too-Busy Silent Majority** reached through low-friction, done-with-you onboarding rather than research-heavy SaaS marketing.
4. **Crew-Control Foreman-Owners** where push-to-talk field capture can become a wedge competitors do not handle naturally.

The common positioning thread across the best segments:

> “Get the job information out of the owner’s head, the crew’s texts, and the glovebox — without forcing a small contractor to become a software company.”
